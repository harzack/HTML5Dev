You add items to a Compound Document the same way you add them to other containers. However, Compound Documents can contain only the following item types:

  • Documents

  • Text Documents

  • Compound Documents

  • Shortcuts to Documents, Text Documents and other Compound Documents

  • Generations of Documents, Text Documents and other Compound Documents

One of the advantages of Compound Documents is that many users can work on different components at the same time. Working on the Documents in a Compound Document is just like working on Documents elsewhere in Content Server; you can use the same reserve, download, and other functions.

When adding a Compound Document to Content Server, you can provide a description of the Compound Document, modify its Categories and Attributes, and add the Compound Document to a different container. For more information about adding items, see Working with General Items.

Each item you add to a Compound Document is numbered sequentially, based on the order in which it is added. However, you can change the order of items at any time. In addition, a Compound Document can include a master document, which you can designate when you reorganize the items in the Compound Document. The master document could be the table of contents of a book, the index page of a Web site, or the cover letter of a report. The master document is always the first element of a Compound Document and the topmost element of its structure.

Content Server does not require sequential numbering. It is perfectly valid to have items numbered 1, 17 and 339. When you reorganize the items, you can have them automatically renumbered to remove any gaps in the sequence.

Versions of Compound Documents are called Releases and Revisions. For more information, see Working with Releases and Revisions.
