You can ask the following types of questions:

  • A system-wide question, which is asked against a topic in the community directory.

  • A community-specific question, which is asked against a community topic in a particular community.

For more information about the community directory, see Using the Community Directory. For more information about community topics, see Browsing Community Pages and Adding Content.

When you ask a question, you specify several settings:

  • A title, which quickly identifies the essence of your question to others. The more precise a title is, the better the answers you will receive.

  • The body of the question, that is, the details of your request for information.

     

    Note:
    An expiration date indicates when the question will close automatically, regardless of question activity. For more information about closing questions, see Working with Questions.

     

  • The urgency, that is, whether you want the question marked urgent.

  • The audience to direct the question to. Audiences consist of users who have subscribed to directories (in the community directory), communities, and forums.

Topics and community topics provide you with two similar ways to target the audience for your questions. Topics organize questions and communities into a hierarchy of concepts within the community directory, which can be viewed by all users. Community topics organize questions and community content into a hierarchy of concepts within a particular community, which can only be viewed and replied to by community members. When you ask a question against a topic, you are asking a system-wide question. When you ask a question against a community topic, you are asking a community-specific question.

When you ask a question, you are automatically subscribed to it. You will receive a question activity report by email when users reply to the question, or when you clarify it, accept or reject its answers, promote its comments to answers, or close it. For more information about question activity, see Working with Questions.
